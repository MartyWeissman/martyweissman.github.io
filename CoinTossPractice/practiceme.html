Coin Flip Probabilities

24 problems on computing probabilities for coin-toss outcomes. Work through each problem, then click Show Answer to check your reasoning.

Part I

Fair Coin

All problems in this section use a fair coin: P(H) = P(T) = 0.5.

01
You flip a fair coin once. What is P(H)?
P(H) = 0.5
A fair coin has two equally likely outcomes. Heads is one of them, so the probability is 0.5 (50%).
02
You flip a fair coin once. What is P(T)?
P(T) = 0.5
By the same reasoning as Problem 1 — or by the complement rule: P(T) = 1 − P(H) = 1 − 0.5 = 0.5.
03
You flip a fair coin twice. What is the probability of getting HT in that exact order?
P(HT) = 0.25
The flips are independent, so P(HT) = P(H) × P(T) = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25. You can also see this by listing all 4 equally likely outcomes for two flips: HH   HT   TH   TT HT is 1 of the 4, so the probability is 0.25.
04
You flip a fair coin twice. What is P(HH)?
P(HH) = 0.25
P(HH) = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25. Same reasoning as Problem 3 — each specific ordered outcome of two fair-coin flips has probability 0.25.
05
You flip a fair coin three times. What is the probability of getting HHT in that exact order?
P(HHT) = 0.125
0.5 × 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.125. With three flips there are 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 equally likely ordered outcomes, and HHT is one of them. So 1 out of 8 = 0.125.
06
You flip a fair coin three times. What is the probability of getting THH in that exact order?
P(THH) = 0.125
Every specific ordered sequence of three fair-coin flips has the same probability: 0.5 × 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.125. The order of H's and T's doesn't affect the probability of any one specific sequence.
07
You flip a fair coin three times. What is the probability of getting exactly two heads (in any order)?
P(exactly 2 H in 3 flips) = 0.375
List out all the sequences with exactly 2 heads: HHT   HTH   THH That's 3 favorable outcomes out of 8 total. Each has probability 0.125, so 3 × 0.125 = 0.375.
08
You flip a fair coin three times. What is the probability of getting exactly one head?
P(exactly 1 H in 3 flips) = 0.375
List out all the sequences with exactly 1 head: HTT   THT   TTH That's 3 favorable outcomes. So 3 × 0.125 = 0.375. Notice this is the same answer as Problem 7! With a fair coin, "exactly k heads" and "exactly k tails" always have the same probability.
09
You flip a fair coin five times. What is the probability that your last flip is heads?
P(last flip is H) = 0.5
Each flip is independent — the coin doesn't "remember" what happened before. The last flip is just a single fair coin flip, so P(H) = 0.5 regardless of how many flips came before it.
10
You flip a fair coin three times. What is the probability of getting at least one head?
P(at least 1 H in 3 flips) = 0.875
Use the complement: the only way to get zero heads is TTT, which has probability 0.5 × 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.125. So P(at least 1 H) = 1 − 0.125 = 0.875.
11
You flip a fair coin four times. What is the probability of getting at least two heads?
P(at least 2 H in 4 flips) = 0.6875
Use the complement: subtract the probability of 0 or 1 heads from 1. With 4 flips there are 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 16 equally likely outcomes, each with probability 0.0625.

0 heads: just one outcome — TTTT. That's 1 × 0.0625 = 0.0625.

1 head: the single H can appear in any of the 4 positions: HTTT   THTT   TTHT   TTTH That's 4 × 0.0625 = 0.25.

So P(at least 2 H) = 1 − 0.0625 − 0.25 = 0.6875.
12
You flip a fair coin five times. What is the probability of getting exactly three heads?
P(exactly 3 H in 5 flips) = 0.3125
With 5 flips there are 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 32 equally likely outcomes. We need to count how many have exactly 3 heads. List them by thinking about where the 2 tails go: HHHTT   HHTHT   HHTTH   HTHHT   HTHTH HTTHH   THHHT   THHTH   THTHH   TTHHH That's 10 outcomes. Each has probability 1/32 = 0.03125, so 10 × 0.03125 = 0.3125.
Part II

Unfair Coins

Each problem specifies its own P(H). The coin is biased — heads and tails are no longer equally likely!

13
P(H) = 0.7
You flip this coin once. What is P(H)?
P(H) = 0.7
This is given directly! The problem is a warm-up to make sure you read the setup: the coin's probability of heads is 0.7 (70%).
14
P(H) = 0.7
You flip this coin once. What is P(T)?
P(T) = 0.3
P(T) = 1 − P(H) = 1 − 0.7 = 0.3. The complement rule works for unfair coins too: the probabilities of all outcomes must add to 1.
15
P(H) = 0.75
You flip this coin twice. What is the probability of getting HT in that exact order?
P(HT) = 0.1875
The flips are independent: P(HT) = P(H) × P(T) = 0.75 × 0.25 = 0.1875. Notice that with an unfair coin, the four possible outcomes (HH, HT, TH, TT) are not equally likely!
16
P(H) = 0.8
You flip this coin twice. What is P(HH)?
P(HH) = 0.64
P(HH) = 0.8 × 0.8 = 0.64. With a strongly biased coin, two heads in a row is actually quite likely — a 64% chance!
17
P(H) = 0.6
You flip this coin twice. What is the probability of getting exactly one head (in any order)?
P(exactly 1 H in 2 flips) = 0.48
Exactly one head means HT or TH. With an unfair coin we compute each separately: P(HT) = 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.24 P(TH) = 0.4 × 0.6 = 0.24 These are mutually exclusive outcomes, so we add: 0.24 + 0.24 = 0.48.
18
P(H) = 0.7
You flip this coin three times. What is the probability of getting exactly two heads?
P(exactly 2 H in 3 flips) = 0.441
List the sequences with exactly 2 heads and compute each probability: P(HHT) = 0.7 × 0.7 × 0.3 = 0.147 P(HTH) = 0.7 × 0.3 × 0.7 = 0.147 P(THH) = 0.3 × 0.7 × 0.7 = 0.147 Each sequence has the same probability (because multiplication can be done in any order), and there are 3 of them: 3 × 0.147 = 0.441.
19
P(H) = 0.2
You flip this coin three times. What is the probability of getting at least one head?
P(at least 1 H in 3 flips) = 0.488
Use the complement: P(at least 1 H) = 1 − P(no heads) = 1 − P(TTT).

P(TTT) = 0.8 × 0.8 × 0.8 = 0.512

So P(at least 1 H) = 1 − 0.512 = 0.488. Even with a coin that only lands heads 20% of the time, three flips give you a nearly 50/50 shot at seeing at least one head!
20
P(H) = 0.75
You flip this coin four times. What is the probability that all four flips are tails?
P(TTTT) ≈ 0.0039
P(TTTT) = 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 × 0.25 = 0.00390625. When heads is very likely (75%), getting four tails in a row is extremely rare — less than a 0.4% chance!
21
P(H) = 0.8
You flip this coin six times. What is the probability that your last flip is heads?
P(last flip is H) = 0.8
Same idea as Problem 9! Each flip is independent — the coin doesn't know or care what happened on flips 1 through 5. The last flip is just a single toss of this coin, so P = 0.8.
22
P(H) = 0.6
You flip this coin four times. What is the probability of getting exactly three heads?
P(exactly 3 H in 4 flips) = 0.3456
List the sequences with exactly 3 heads — the single T can go in any of the 4 positions: P(HHHT) = 0.6 × 0.6 × 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.0864 P(HHTH) = 0.6 × 0.6 × 0.4 × 0.6 = 0.0864 P(HTHH) = 0.6 × 0.4 × 0.6 × 0.6 = 0.0864 P(THHH) = 0.4 × 0.6 × 0.6 × 0.6 = 0.0864 All 4 have the same probability, so 4 × 0.0864 = 0.3456.
23
P(H) = 0.25
You flip this coin four times. What is the probability of getting at least two heads?
P(at least 2 H in 4 flips) ≈ 0.2617
Use the complement: subtract P(0 heads) and P(1 head) from 1.

0 heads: P(TTTT) = 0.75 × 0.75 × 0.75 × 0.75 ≈ 0.3164.

1 head: The single H can go in any of the 4 positions: HTTT   THTT   TTHT   TTTH Each has probability 0.25 × 0.75 × 0.75 × 0.75 ≈ 0.1055. There are 4 such sequences, so 4 × 0.1055 ≈ 0.4219.

P(at least 2 H) = 1 − 0.3164 − 0.4219 ≈ 0.2617.
24
P(H) ≈ 0.333
You flip this coin five times. What is the probability of getting exactly four heads?
P(exactly 4 H in 5 flips) ≈ 0.0412
The single T can go in any of the 5 positions: HHHHT   HHHTH   HHTHH   HTHHH   THHHH Each sequence has the same probability: 0.333 × 0.333 × 0.333 × 0.333 × 0.667 ≈ 0.00823 There are 5 such sequences, so 5 × 0.00823 ≈ 0.0412. Getting 4 heads out of 5 flips with a coin that only lands heads about a third of the time is quite unlikely — about a 4% chance.
